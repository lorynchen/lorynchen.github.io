Walmart Shopper Performance

Empowering Personal Shoppers to pick faster.

Walmart has been aggressively expanding its grocery pickup and delivery services to its 4700+ stores in the US. To help with that, we have an app called GIF (Global Integrated Fulfillment) that store associates use to fulfill grocery orders.

As part of the Grocery team, I led the design of a new feature that helped Personal Shoppers better manage their time and fulfill orders faster.

THE PROBLEM

How might we enable Walmart Personal Shoppers to fulfill orders faster while on their pick walks?

Data showed us that there were a lot of low-performing associates for order fulfillment across stores in the nation. This negatively affected store's pick rates.

As Walmart's Grocery Pickup and Delivery entered a strong growth stage, it became clear we had to address this issue now before scaling operations to new markets.

BACKGROUND INFO

Key terminology to know

Personal Shopper - A Walmart associate that fulfills online orders by gathering items around the sales floor of the store.

Pick Walk - One round of picking orders. Usually takes about an hour.

Pick Rate - Number of items an associate picks, over on hour.

Store Pick Rate - Average of individual Pick Rates for a given store.

EVIDENCE

How do we know this is a real problem?

I first began looking through our qualitative feedback from Walmart Personal Shoppers.

A good chunk of the issues were related to the app not providing enough performance information to help the associate better manage their time.

With store pick rates already being low, I saw this as an opportunity to experiment and see if we could increase individual pick rates and in turn, store pick rates, all while providing a better experience for our associates.

💡 OPPORTUNITY

By providing relevant performance information to the personal shopper on their pick walk, we'll give them a strong benchmark to either speed up or maintain their current rate.

RESEARCH

Livin' like a Personal Shopper

To start things off, a few of us went into stores and worked as a grocery store associate.

We aimed to understand their day-to-day, their emotions, and how their picking operations were constantly being handled.

This helped me empathize for our associates and allowed me see things from their perspective.

Three things I learned:

  1. Associates are working 8 hours a day doing repetitive, manual tasks. How can we boost their motivation?
  2. For every pick walk, there's an optimal time the associate needs to pick their items in. This depends on the item, number of items, and its location.
  3. Walmarts are always busy. Like really busy. Associates have to maneuver around the store, help customers, clean messes, all while fulfilling an order. How can we provide value while not distracting them from their work.

I began exploring a ton of different concepts.

USABILITY TESTING

Concepts 1, 2, and 4

After design reviews, I decided to move forward with three concepts: Concepts 1, 2, and 4. I updated these three concepts and tested them with real shoppers in their real environments.

I had them go on a real pick walk using my prototype and tested the designs. Here's what I learned:

ITERATIONS

As a direct reaction to the feedback, I updated Concept 4, utilizing various positive messages to cycle through to the associate while removing the negatives. I also updated the next Item card to work with a new set of information on the screen.

This was an approach my engineering partners, PM, and I agreed upon as a way to boost motivation and provide more transparency on their pick walk performance.

SECOND TESTING

Simulating a real pick walk (again)

I felt pretty good about this design, however, I still wanted to test with associates.

I tested with two person shoppers by simulating a real pick walk in a store. I had an associate use their phones with the prototype pulled up. We got our carts, and items for the walk.

I looked at 3 important things:

  1. Time it takes to complete a pick walk.
  2. Number of wrong items picked.
  3. Overall feeling of the experience.

LEARNINGS

  • Shoppers felt really good when presented positive messaging.
  • The time to complete pick walks actually decreased from last time.
  • They loved the Pick due time displayed because it gives them a benchmark to work with.
  • Overall positive experience.

LAUNCH

Feelin' good, like I should

This new feature launched in a few stores and was met with a positive outcome.

Calling attention to when you're overdue

Don't worry! Associates fulfill orders 5-6 hours ahead of time. So, being overdue doesn't mean you're late.

It means you're past Walmart's recommended pick time which is part of the guidelines to alert you.

OUTCOME

  • 10% increase in store pick rates.
  • Pick rates for low-performing associates increased by 17%.
  • A boost in positive experiences for associates.

KEY TAKEAWAYS

Focus on the user and business will follow

What started from a business problem, quickly turned into a focus on a particular user pain point. Because of this, I believe we were able to design the best solution to meet our business goals.

Don't overlook emotional design

"People will forget what you said. People will forget what you did. But, people will never forget how you made them feel." I believe the same is true for apps.

Building something that speaks to people on an emotional level is what makes a high-quality experience. This project was a stepping stone for our team to build a more emotional product for our associates.
